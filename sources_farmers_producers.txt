Local Farmers & Ingredient Producers: Knowledge, Heritage, and Preservation 

 

Brown, Cheryl, and Stacy Miller. “The Impacts of Local Markets: A Review of Research on Farmers Markets and Community Supported Agriculture (CSA).” American Journal of Agricultural Economics, vol. 90, no. 5, 2008, pp. 1296–1302. 

Brown and Miller analyze research on farmers markets and Community Supported Agriculture (CSA) programs, arguing that local food systems strengthen both rural economies and community identity. They explain how direct-to-consumer markets allow farmers to keep more profit while also building trust with customers. Instead of being anonymous suppliers, farmers become visible and valued members of the community. The article emphasizes that these systems protect small-scale producers from being pushed out by large industrial agriculture. For my stakeholder perspective, this source helps me frame local farmers as cultural keepers, not just business owners. In our project about bourbon and Kentucky food, this research supports the idea that when local producers disappear, communities lose more than products—they lose knowledge, stories, and traditions tied to the land. 

 

La Trobe, Helen. “Farmers’ Markets: Consuming Local Rural Produce.” International Journal of Consumer Studies, vol. 25, no. 3, 2001, pp. 181–192. 

La Trobe explores why consumers are drawn to farmers markets, especially concerns about food safety, sustainability, and authenticity. She argues that farmers markets shorten the distance between producer and consumer, creating transparency and accountability in the food system. The article also discusses environmental benefits, such as reduced food miles and seasonal eating habits. What stands out is the emphasis on place-based food practices—foods that are tied to specific regions and traditions. This connects directly to my topic because local Kentucky ingredients play a major role in shaping bourbon culture and regional identity. I will use this source to support the idea that when production becomes industrialized or disconnected from local farms, cultural meaning can weaken. It helps show why preserving local ingredient producers matters beyond economics and how it matters for identity. 

 

Toler, Stephen, et al. “Fairness, Farmers Markets, and Local Production.” American Journal of Agricultural Economics, vol. 91, no. 5, 2009, pp. 1272–1278. 

Toler and his co-authors study how ideas of fairness influence consumer support for local food. Through experimental research, they show that many consumers are willing to pay more when they believe farmers are treated fairly and receive proper compensation. The article introduces the idea that people care about ethical food systems, not just taste or price. This perspective is important because it highlights how commercialization can either support or exploit local producers. For my stakeholder focus, this source helps me argue that fairness is part of preserving tradition. If bourbon tourism and food commercialization prioritize profit over producers, local farmers may lose control over their craft and knowledge. I will use this research to raise questions in our storytelling project about who benefits from Kentucky’s food culture and whether farmers’ voices remain central or become overshadowed. 

 

Monamodi, Prayer, et al. “The Impact of Innovative Irrigation System Use on Crop Yield Among Smallholder Farmers in Mbombela Local Municipality, South Africa.” Agriculture, vol. 15, 2025. 

This peer-reviewed study examines how innovative irrigation systems affect crop yields among smallholder farmers. Using statistical analysis, the authors find that farmers who adopt modern irrigation technologies experience significantly higher productivity. The study also shows that age, income, and production costs influence who adopts these technologies. Although this research focuses on South Africa, it offers insight into how modernization impacts agricultural traditions. In my project, I use this source to explore the tension between preserving traditional methods and adapting to change. Technology can increase efficiency, but it can also shift practices away from older, hand-taught methods. In the context of Kentucky farmers and ingredient producers, this research helps me discuss how modernization may protect livelihoods while also transforming cultural knowledge. 

 

Kebede, Elias Gudissa, and Mehari Getaneh. “Land Dispute Settlement between Farmers and Local Administration: Expropriation, Compensation and Asymmetry of Power.” African Journal on Land Policy and Geospatial Sciences, vol. 8, no. 7, 2025. 

Kebede and Getaneh investigate land disputes between farmers and local governments, focusing on issues of compensation and power imbalance. Their findings show that farmers often receive inadequate compensation when land is expropriated, and they face long legal battles that weaken their economic stability. The article highlights how land ownership is directly connected to livelihood, identity, and generational knowledge. Even though this study is not set in Kentucky, it reinforces a broader idea that when farmers lose land, communities lose traditions tied to that land. For my stakeholder perspective, this source supports the argument that commercialization and expansion such as tourism development can threaten small producers. If farmers are displaced or pressured economically, the handwritten and orally passed-down traditions they carry may disappear. 

 

Pszczola, Donald E. “What Makes a Winning Ingredient?” Food Technology, Aug. 2012, pp. 58–65. 

Pszczola reviews ingredient innovations introduced at a food industry expo, focusing on how companies modify traditional ingredients to meet market demands. The article discusses sodium reduction, protein reformulation, and other technological adjustments designed to increase efficiency and appeal. While the article celebrates innovation, it also reveals how food production increasingly prioritizes scalability and commercial success. This source helps me contrast industrial ingredient production with local, small-scale farming practices. In Kentucky’s bourbon and food culture, ingredients are deeply connected to land and tradition. When ingredients become standardized or reformulated for mass production, some of that connection can fade. I will use this article to show how commercialization may unintentionally replace handwritten or generational knowledge with uniform, market-driven processes.
