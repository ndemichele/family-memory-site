Annotated Bibliography
“Featured Story: Bourbon + Tourism = Bourbonism.” The Lane Report, 17 Sept. 2021,
www.lanereport.com/146389/2021/09/featured-story-bourbon-tourism-bourbonism/.
This article from The Lane Report breaks down the economic relationship
between bourbon and tourism in Kentucky, coining the whole thing
“bourbonism.” It covers how Louisville rebranded itself as “Bourbon City” and
saw visitation nearly double from 11 million in 2007 to over 19 million in 2019.
The piece includes perspectives from tourism officials, distillery representatives,
and economic development leaders who all frame bourbon tourism as a
collaborative ecosystem rather than a competition between brands. It also touches
on how distilleries adapted during COVID with virtual tours and digital
experiences to keep the brand alive even when people couldn’t visit in person.
This source bridges the gap between the academic research and the industry
marketing by showing how bourbonism plays out in real economic terms. It gives
my argument concrete numbers and quotes from people inside the tourism and
distilling worlds, which strengthens both the logos and ethos of my argument. I’ll
use it alongside Smith’s historical analysis to show how bourbon went from
regional product to full-blown economic engine, and how distillers depend on that
tourism pipeline to sustain growth.

“Impact.” Kentucky Distillers’ Association, kybourbon.com/industry/impact/.
This page from the KDA lays out the raw economic numbers behind bourbon’s
footprint in Kentucky. It reports that bourbon is a $9 billion industry generating
over 23,000 jobs with annual payroll of $1.63 billion and $358 million in state
and local tax revenue. The page also highlights that over 74% of Bourbon Trail
visitors come from out of state and that distillers are in the middle of a $5.4
billion capital investment spree. It frames bourbon not just as a product but as the
backbone of Kentucky’s economy, connecting distilling to farming, construction,
forestry, and hospitality in a way that makes the whole state dependent on the
industry’s success. This source works as the numbers-heavy complement to the
more narrative sources in my bibliography. Where Smith gives historical context
and The Lane Report tells the tourism story, this page delivers the hard stats that
make the economic argument undeniable. I’ll use it to reinforce the logos appeal
of my distiller stakeholder position by showing exactly how much money, how
many jobs, and how much tax revenue bourbon puts into Kentucky communities.
It also strengthens ethos because the data comes directly from the industry’s own
trade organization.

Kentucky Distillers’ Association. “Kentucky Bourbon Trail®.” Kentucky Bourbon Trail,
kybourbontrail.com/.
This source is basically the industry’s own pitch for what the Kentucky Bourbon
Trail® is and why it matters. The KDA highlights the trail’s history, economic
impact, and how it’s been branded as a cultural experience and not just a tour. The
site leans heavily into words like craftsmanship, tradition, and heritage while also
pointing to visitor numbers and revenue growth. It’s interesting because it’s
marketing bourbon as this authentic local tradition while also clearly operating as
a large-scale tourism machine at the same time. This is the industry speaking
directly, which makes it really useful for analyzing how distillers frame
authenticity in a commercial setting. When I put this next to Smith’s historical
analysis, it helps show the gap between what bourbon actually was for workers
and communities versus how it’s being packaged and sold to tourists today. That
tension adds a lot of depth to my stakeholder argument.
DeMichele 4
Smith, Frederick H. Bourbon Empire: The Past and Future of America’s Whiskey. University
Press of Kentucky, 2013.
In this book, Frederick H. Smith breaks down how Kentucky bourbon went from
something people were making on the frontier to a global luxury product people
actually pay a lot of money for. He ties bourbon’s growth to Kentucky’s regional
identity, farming, and big economic shifts like Prohibition and industrialization.
One of his main points is how the industry went from small family distilleries to
being run by big corporations, but they still rely heavily on stories about tradition
and authenticity to sell it. Basically, bourbon isn’t just a drink. It’s a symbol that
was shaped by land, labor, and smart marketing. This source gives my argument
some academic weight and a solid historical foundation for the bourbon distiller
stakeholder. It helps explain the tension distillers deal with between honoring
tradition and making money off of it. I’ll use Smith’s analysis to back up points
about how heritage branding drives tourism and global sales, which strengthens
both the logos and ethos of my argument.
DeMichele 5
Verhaal, J. Cameron, and Glenn R. Carroll. “Authenticity among Distilleries: Signaling,
Transparency, and Essence.” Poetics, vol. 95, 2022.
In this peer-reviewed study, Verhaal and Carroll dig into what actually makes
consumers see a distillery as authentic. They ran multiple studies including
surveys, experiments, and reviews to figure out which features of a distillery
make people trust the brand. Their big finding is that visible, costly investments
like having on-site stills and growing your own grain matter way more to
consumers than intangible stuff like family stories or master distiller lore. They
also found that outsourcing production is basically a death sentence for a brand’s
authenticity. The study frames authenticity not as something a distillery just has,
but as something it has to actively build and prove through real, observable
commitment to the craft. This source is gold for backing up the distiller
stakeholder argument because it gives hard data on why heritage branding works
the way it does. It connects directly to what the KDA is doing with the Bourbon
Trail by showing that the “authentic experience” distillers sell tourists isn’t just
marketing fluff. It actually shapes how people evaluate the product. I’ll use this to
strengthen the logos of my argument by grounding claims about branding strategy
in peer-reviewed research.
